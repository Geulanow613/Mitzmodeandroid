  [11] Learn the laws of lashon hara (evil speech) from the Chofetz Chaim. -Submitted by Moshe. Our sages compare harmful speech to the most serious sins — and teach that habitual gossip can destroy relationships, communities, and a person's own spiritual standing. The Chofetz Chaim devoted his life to showing how the Torah warns about speech again and again, because words can build worlds or tear them down. Even one day of guarding your tongue can transform how you see other people — and how they see you.
  [12] Eat some chametz (leavened food) and have in mind that you're fulfilling the mitzvah of cleaning your home of chametz for Pesach. If it is currently Pesach, eat some matzah! On the night before Pesach, Jewish families search every corner of the home by candlelight for any last crumb of chametz — because no leaven may remain in the house during the seven days of the holiday. We left Egypt in such a rush that our bread had no time to rise, and clearing out chametz each year reconnects us to that moment of freedom. What you're chewing today is part of that ancient story, if you have in mind you're doing it in honor of the holiday.
  [13] Give the next Jewish person you see a blessing. If you can't think of anything, just bless them that they should have a long and healthy life! In the Temple, the Kohanim would raise their hands and bless the entire nation with words of peace and protection that we still hear in synagogue today. You don't have to be a Kohen to wish someone well — a heartfelt blessing from an ordinary Jew is a beautiful act of love. When you bless another person, you become a channel for good in the world.
  [17] Actively oppose idolatry! 🔨 The Rambam explains in Chapter 7 that we have a positive commandment to destroy items of idol worship and everything used for it. Here's something powerful: We don't just avoid idol worship - we actively work to eliminate it! Did you know? Even the wood and stones from an idol must be destroyed. Today's mission: Take an active stand against negative influences in your life.

  [9] Pray for a refuah shleimah (complete healing) for Raphael Yosef ben Rachel. When praying for someone who is ill, we use their Hebrew name together with their mother's Hebrew name. According to rabbinic tradition (such as in the Talmud), mothers are often viewed as having fewer spiritual liabilities and possess immense grace, making their merit powerful when praying for a speedy recovery.
  [11] Get a Chumash and open to a random page and verse — Submitted by HB. Many Jews have the cherished custom of opening the Torah to seek inspiration or guidance at important moments in life. Whether you see the verse as a personal message or simply as a spark for reflection, the act itself connects you to Torah — and our sages teach that even studying a single verse is its own mitzvah. Read what you land on slowly, think about what it might mean for your life right now, and let the words sink in.
  [20] Spend the next 30 seconds thanking G-d for everything you could possibly thank Him for. - Submitted by MB. After the Jewish people ate and were satisfied in the wilderness, Moses taught them to bless G-d — establishing gratitude as a fundamental part of Jewish life. Our sages ask: Who is truly rich? The one who is happy with what he already has. You don't need a long prayer — just pause and name the gifts in front of you right now: your breath, your eyes, someone you love, the food on your table.
  [22] Clean your bathroom extra spick and span in honor of Shabbat. - Submitted by AS. Our sages teach that preparing for Shabbat is itself a mitzvah — and that whoever toils on Friday will enjoy the rewards on Shabbat. A beautiful midrash describes how two angels accompany a person home from synagogue on Friday night: one good, one bad. If they find the home ready — candles lit, table set— the good angel blesses the home for the next Shabbat to be the same and the bad angel is forced to say 'amen'. Honoring Shabbat means making the whole home feel like a palace for the King.
  [23] Pray for a refuah shleimah (complete healing) for Sarah Dina bat Odel - submitted by DM. When praying for someone who is ill, we use their Hebrew name together with their mother's Hebrew name — because mystical teachings, like those in the Zohar, suggest that the soul’s deepest spiritual essence is intimately connected to the mother. Using the mother's name is meant to appeal to divine compassion and maternal nurturing energy.

  [2] Pray for a shidduch (wife) for my friend Mordechai Shaul ben Devorah - submitted by HS. Our sages teach that when you pray for another person and you happen to need the same thing yourself, you are answered first — because selfless prayer opens the highest gates. They also teach that matching a husband and wife is as difficult for G-d as splitting the Red Sea, meaning every marriage is orchestrated from Heaven with extraordinary care. A heartfelt prayer for a friend's shidduch is one of the greatest gifts you can give.
  [3] Got some spare time? Visit someone who's sick. Our sages teach that visiting the sick removes one-sixtieth of their illness! The mitzvah of bikkur cholim — visiting the sick — is one of the ways we walk in G-d's ways, just as He visited Abraham when he was recovering. Jewish law teaches that we should sit with the ill, comfort them, and help with whatever they need — even if it's just keeping them company for a few minutes. Your presence can lift someone's spirits as much as any medicine.
  [4] Saw a lost object recently? 🔍 Don't just ignore it! Go back and try to return it to its owner (if possible). That's the mitzvah of Hashavat Aveidah (returning lost objects). The Torah teaches that if you see your fellow's animal or belongings wandering off, you may not look away — you must make an effort to return them. Our sages devoted entire discussions to this mitzvah: how hard you must try, what you can keep as a reward for your trouble, and why restoring someone's property is also restoring their dignity.
  [5] Pray for Moshiach!!!!!!! - submitted by EV. Belief in the coming of Mashiach is one of the thirteen foundations of Jewish faith — we anticipate a king from the house of David who will rebuild the Temple, gather the exiles, and fill the world with knowledge of G-d. Our sages teach that a Jew must live every day with the expectation of redemption. When you pray for Mashiach, you express hope for a world of peace, holiness, and the complete revelation of G-d's presence.
  [6] Take a minute to pray for someone else's needs before your own. Our sages teach a remarkable secret: when you pray for another person and you happen to need the same thing yourself, you are answered first. Putting someone else's needs before your own transforms prayer from a personal wish list into an act of love for the entire Jewish people.
  [7] Try to make peace between two people you know who are currently arguing. The reward for doing so is very great! Hillel taught us to be among the disciples of Aaron — a lover of peace and a pursuer of peace, who would greet every person warmly and draw them closer to Torah. Our sages teach that peace is so precious that G-d allows His own holy Name to be erased in order to restore peace between a husband and wife. Helping two people reconcile may be one of the greatest acts of kindness in the world.
  [9] Call someone who is lonely. Submitted by: Amy. Our sages warn never to separate yourself from the community — because a Jew needs connection to thrive. Visiting the sick removes one-sixtieth of their suffering, and loneliness can be its own kind of illness. A phone call, a message, or a visit tells someone they matter — and loving your fellow Jew is one of the foundations of everything we stand for.
