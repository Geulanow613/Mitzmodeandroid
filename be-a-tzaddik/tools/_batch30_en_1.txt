Learn about Shemittah — the seventh year when the land of Israel rests — and what this actually means for Jews living in Israel today! 🌾 Every seven years, the Torah commands that the land of Israel must rest: no plowing, planting, pruning, or commercial harvesting. Generally you're only allowed to do basic things to keep plants alive but not to improve them. Produce that grows on its own during Shemittah has 'kedushat shevi'it' (seventh-year sanctity) and can't be sold normally. The challenge: how does a country of millions survive without farming for a year? Two main halachic solutions are used today in Israel: (1) 'Heter Mechira' — a legal sale of Israeli farmland to a non-Jew for the year, so technically a non-Jew 'owns' the land during Shemittah and can farm it. The Chief Rabbinate permits this; many orthodox authorities do not. (2) 'Otzar Beit Din' — a rabbinical court takes formal ownership of the crops and distributes them, charging only for labor costs, not for the produce itself. Products with Otzar Beit Din certification need to be treated with Shemittah sanctity in your home (eaten and not wasted or destroyed). If the produce rots on its own that's fine, but you can't add it to the garbage and thus intentionally make it inedible. Many people use special "Shemittah bins" that let the food decompose naturally if they're not going to eat it. Authorities debate whether it's a positive mitzvah to enjoy holy Shemittah fruit.
